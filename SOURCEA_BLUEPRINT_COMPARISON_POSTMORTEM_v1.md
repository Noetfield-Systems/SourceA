# PART B — POST-MORTEM: OLD v1 PLANS

> **Method:** Imagine it is December 2026. The v1 plans were executed as written. What went wrong? What went right? What was the cause?

---

## What Worked in v1 (Would Have Survived)

### ✅ Win conditions were correctly defined
W1/W2/W3 with PASS criteria were the strongest part of both v1 documents. They survived unchanged into v2. Specific, binary, non-negotiable. This framing was correct and would not have needed revision.

### ✅ Critical path diagnosis was accurate
"The bottleneck is not the demo. It is the outreach start date." v1-B stated this in Week 1. This was the highest-value strategic insight in both documents and it was correct. Every week W3 starts late = one week less recovery time. This survived into v2.

### ✅ DELETE list was strong
The 7-item forbidden list in v1-B was operationally correct. It named the exact failure modes (Trust OS naming, architecture expansion, category debate) before they happened. The Frozen Zone in v2 is an extension of this, not a replacement.

### ✅ Probability regime was honest
<10% for $100M, 30-40% for seed. This was the correct framing. It prevented false optimism from dominating the execution plan. v2 preserved it.

### ✅ "If it can bypass, it doesn't exist"
The investor one-liner from v1-B is more punchy than the longer commercial sentence in v2. It was correct as a pitch instrument. It should not have been retired — it should coexist alongside the longer sales sentence.

### ✅ Demo scenario was precisely specified
v1-A had exact beats (BLOCK/ALLOW/TAMPER/KILL), exact timing (30s/60s/60s/30s), exact terminal commands (D3-A, D5, etc.), and a dry run requirement. This was the most executable engineering spec in either document.

---

## What Failed in v1 (Root Causes)

### ❌ Engineering was the organizing spine — commercial was secondary
Both v1 documents led with engineering weeks. Commercial was a column in the table. The implicit priority signal was: build first, sell later. This is exactly the failure mode v2 corrects. By Week 12 of the v1 plan, the founder would still be hardening the demo while W3 had been on the plan for 12 weeks but was not prioritized.

**Root cause:** Document architecture. A table with "Engineering Deliverable" as the first column and "Commercial Action" as the fourth column communicates priority through structure, not just words.

### ❌ Energy allocation was never stated
Neither v1 document said "spend 45% of your time on W3." They said "commercial starts week 1" but without an energy allocation law, a solo founder naturally gravitates toward the work that feels more controllable (engineering). There was no enforcement mechanism for time distribution.

**Root cause:** Absence of a governing constraint. The DELETE list told workers what not to build, but nothing told the founder how to spend their hours.

### ❌ Two documents created authority fragmentation
v1-A was filed as the master plan. v1-B was filed as the VC roadmap. They overlapped significantly, had different organizing principles, and were both annotated with "SUPERSEDED" notes within 24 hours of being written. Any agent reading either document could not tell which one was authoritative.

**Root cause:** Architecture of governance. SourceA's SSOT law says disk state > conversation. But having two disk documents with different structures for the same mission created the exact fragmentation the law was designed to prevent.

### ❌ No explicit identity separation (internal vs external)
Both v1 documents used "AI Governance Execution Layer" as the identity — a fine internal label but one that creates diligence risk when used externally. The documents never distinguished between what you say to an investor and what you call the system internally. This would have produced inconsistent messaging across 26 weeks.

**Root cause:** The documents were written from inside the system. The external perspective (what does a buyer need to hear?) was an afterthought, not a governing constraint.

### ❌ The $2K question was never answered
Neither document answered: "What expensive problem disappears within 30 days if a customer pays CAD $2,000?" The closest was the outreach line ("We make AI execution impossible to bypass governance — 15-min pilot call?") but that describes the system, not the problem it solves for the buyer.

**Root cause:** Engineering-first thinking. The documents described what the system does, not what the buyer gets. A buyer does not buy features. A buyer buys problem elimination.

### ❌ Demo spec would have been rebuilt
The extremely detailed demo spec (D1-D6, speaker notes, exact timing) in v1-A was correct but fragile. It assumed weeks 1-6 of engineering were not yet done. They were already done (system note voided weeks 1-6 the same day). The engineering sequence would have been rebuilt from Week 7 onward with no clear spec for what a "film-ready" demo actually required at that point.

**Root cause:** Filing a plan and then voiding the first 6 weeks on the same day creates an orphaned document. The spirit survived but the executable steps did not.

---

## Post-Mortem Verdict on v1

> **v1 plans were strategically correct but architecturally self-defeating.** The right ideas (W3 early, demo BLOCK/ALLOW/TAMPER, honest probability regime) were present. The execution structure (engineering-first table, no energy law, two competing documents, no buyer answer) would have caused the founder to allocate time incorrectly regardless of what the documents said.

## Failure Scenario 1 — The Document Became Another Governance Artifact
**Probability: 45%**

**How to detect early:** If by Day 3 the first outreach email has not been sent, this scenario is in progress.

**Prevention:** Add a Day 1 Action Card (not part of v2). One page. One action. "Send this email today."

---

## Failure Scenario 2 — Frozen Zone Is Not Enforced Because There Is No Enforcer
**Probability: 40%**

What happens: By Week 3, a GPT paste introduces a new concept ("Causal Receipt Graph" or "Policy Enforcement Layer v2"). It does not use any of the exact frozen zone terms. Maintainer 2 builds it because the task description passes the literal frozen zone check. Three weeks of engineering on something that does not increase W1, W2, or W3.

**Why:** The frozen zone in v2 is a list of forbidden names. A new concept with a new name bypasses the list. The v1 DELETE list had the same vulnerability.

**How to detect early:** Any new script file that is not on the K1-K8 list.

**Prevention:** The gate test should be: "Does this task appear on the K1-K8 list or serve a named W1/W2/W3 directly?" If no → reject. Not "is this name on the frozen list?"

---

## Failure Scenario 3 — The Commercial Sentence Gets Simplified Wrong Under Pressure
**Probability: 35%**

What happens: ASF gets on a Zoom call with TF. The 3-sentence commercial answer is too long to deliver naturally. Under pressure, ASF reverts to architecture language ("we have a governance runtime with event spine..."). The buyer disconnects.

**Why:** The commercial sentence in v2 is actually two sentences, not one. It requires rehearsal. Without rehearsal, it becomes architecture description under pressure.

**How to detect early:** First call debrief — did the buyer ask "so what does this do for me?" If yes, the commercial sentence was not delivered.

**Prevention:** Practice the sentence out loud 10 times before the first call. Record yourself. If you can't say it naturally in 20 seconds, shorten it further.

## Failure Scenario 4 — Energy Allocation Law Has No Measurement
**Probability: 50%**

What happens: The 45/25/15/10/5 law is stated in v2. There is no measurement mechanism. At the end of Week 2, the actual allocation was 10% Revenue / 60% Kernel / 20% Demo / 10% Other. Nobody caught it because nobody measured it.

**Why:** A law without enforcement is a suggestion. v2 states the law correctly but provides no daily or weekly check-in mechanism to verify compliance.

**How to detect early:** Weekly 5-minute audit: "How many hours did I spend on outreach vs engineering this week?"

**Prevention:** Add a weekly energy allocation self-audit. Two questions: (1) Hours on W3 this week? (2) Hours on engineering this week? If ratio is inverted, freeze engineering for 24 hours.

## Failure Scenario 5 — The v2 Blueprint Has No Bridge to brain-os/

A new Maintainer session reads brain-os/ first (correct read order), gets old architectural context, then reads v2, and tries to reconcile the two. **How to detect early:** If Maintainer produces a "synthesis" or "reconciliation" document before Week 1 engineering starts.

**Prevention:** Add one explicit line to v2: "This document governs commercial strategy and execution priority. brain-os/ governs architecture laws. Where they conflict: brain-os/ wins on architecture; this document wins on execution priority and energy allocation."

## Failure Scenario 6 — Demo Spec in v2 Is Weaker Than v1
